6. Numerical experiments
In this section, we provide a comprehensive experimental analysis that ex-
plores the efficiency and effectiveness of our models, methodologies, and algo-
rithms. In Section 6.1, we begin by introducing the real-world instances used
for our experiments. In Section 6.2, we present the searchability and perfor-
mance of the BPC algorithm outlined in Section 4 and the VNS metaheuristic
described in Section 5 in solving real-world instances. We then present a sensi-
tivity analysis and derive pragmatic managerial insights in Section 6.3.
We implement the designed algorithm in Python 3.7.12 and utilize Gurobi
Optimizer v10.0.3rc0 (Windows 64-bit) with a single thread to solve the LP
and MIP models. All experiments were performed on a laptop PC with an Intel
Core i7-10750H CPU and 16GB RAM under Windows 11 (64-bit version).
6.1. Datasets and methods
The real-world dataset is sourced from our industry partner,Sanyangma, a
prominent TC based in Chongqing, China. In daily operations, the company
mainly utilizes railway transportation with JSQ6 wagons for automobile dis-
tribution. The data, covering the period from June 2017 to September 2018,
includes 1,370 orders and 148,087 automobiles for assignment and loading with
JSQ6 wagons. Data fields encompass detailed schedule information, including
wagon assignments, automobile models and locations, deck positions, frequency
of loading and unloading, and any delays or disruptions encountered during op-
erations.
We collect instances at three scales (small, medium, and large) based on the
number of automobiles|I|ranging from 5 to 15, and the number of wagons
|J |ranging from 7 to 11. The number of optional and mandatory automobiles
varies from 90 to 250 and 30 to 80, respectively. Each scale is allocated 10
instances, resulting in a testbed comprising a total of 30 (10×3) instances.
We consider a fixed clearance of∆ = 0.15m to prevent automobile scratches
in real-world applications. All of the above parameters have been validated by
our partners. The time limit for heuristics is set at 900 seconds, whereas for
the Gurobi solver and BPC solution process, it is set at 3,600 seconds (denoted
as T.L.). Utilizing the real-world testbeds, we tested the following methods in
our experiments:
i. BI: The best insertion algorithm presented in Section 5.
ii. VNS: The variable neighborhood search algorithm presented in Section 5.
iii. GRB: Using the Gurobi solver to solve the compact formulation in Section
3.
iv. BPC BI: The BPC algorithm developed in Section 4, adopting the BI
algorithm to warm up.

6.3.1. Comparison of different objectives
Specific to our problem, the primary goal is to maximize wagon utilization.
However, how to achieve this is controversial due to the existence of two distinct
objectives: automobile loading quantityObjQtyand automobile loading length
ObjLen. Intuitively, since the former may lead to the excessive selection of
smaller automobiles, the latter is more likely a reasonable choice. However, the
practitioners and administrators prefer theObjQtyfor ease of interpretation
in their operations. Fortunately, our model remains tractable for both of the
objectives. To answer their questions and provide practical scheduling recom-
mendations, in what ensues, we test on all instances to examine the impact
of these two objectives on the performance of our model and algorithms. The
results are presented in Table H.9.
Upon examining the results, as expected, it is evident that the length ob-
jective achieves significant improvements over the quantity objective. As de-
picted in Fig. 6a, for small-scale instances, the solution ofObjQtyshows a
7.8% improvement in loading quantity compared to that ofObjLen. However,
as the problem scale expands to medium or larger, the loading quantities are
nearly identical for both objectives. Conversely, the loading lengths show a
marked difference across all scales. Notably, the objectiveObjLenachieves as
much as almost 12-meter improvement in total loading length for instances with
equivalent loading quantities. This advantage becomes more pronounced as the
problem scale increases, since the solution space expands.
The above observations can be attributed to the degeneracy inherent in
the optimal solutions underObjQty. Specifically, when the residual idle space
within the schedule is insufficient to accommodate an additional unit, the non-
uniqueness of theObjQty-optimal solutions may lead to the selection of a
suboptimal loading arrangement inObjLen, thereby compromising the over-
all loading efficiency. This analysis also explains whyObjLenrequires more
computational time to deliver a better solution. From a long-term operational
perspective,ObjLenproves more effective for managing automobile composi-
tion. This is intuitive asObjQtytends to prioritize small-size automobiles,
potentially causing automobile composition imbalance and comparable cumu-
lative resource wastage, as we shall see in the next section. Consequently,
practitioners and administrators are ultimately convinced to adoptObjLenfor
its operational advantages.
Beyond these two objectives, TCs may progressively incorporate additional
managerial goals, such as revenue maximization or cost minimization, as their
operational systems evolve. The proposed optimization method exhibits sig-
nificant potential for multi-objective optimization to obtain the Pareto-optimal
solution,particularlywhendispatchersmustsimultaneouslyaddressspatialcon-
